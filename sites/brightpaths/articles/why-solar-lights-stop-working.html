Why Do Solar Lights Stop Working?

Short answer: in most cases the battery has worn out, the panel is dirty or shaded, or the light sensor is being fooled by nearby light sources. Work through the causes below in order — they run from most to least likely, and the first two account for the majority of "dead" solar lights.

1. The battery has aged out

The rechargeable cell cycles from full to empty essentially every day, and that daily cycling wears chemistry out long before the LED or panel degrades — the pattern explained in how long solar lights last. Symptoms: the light runs dimmer and shorter each month, then quits. Test: if the fixture takes a standard replaceable cell, swap in a fresh rechargeable of the same type and chemistry. If the light revives, you are done, at a fraction of the cost of replacement.

2. The panel is dirty or newly shaded

A film of dust, pollen, or hard-water spots blocks the light the panel needs, and a season of plant growth can shade a spot that was sunny when you installed. Symptoms: weak output after cloudy months, or a light that used to last all night fading by midnight. Test: wipe the panel clean, check the spot at midday, and relocate the stake if anything now casts shade on it.

3. The sensor thinks it is still daytime

Solar lights switch on when the panel stops producing — darkness is the signal. A porch lamp, street light, or even a bright neighboring window can hold the sensor just above its threshold so the light never turns on. Test: cover the panel completely with your hand after dark. If the LED lights up, the fixture is fine; the location is the problem.

4. Moisture has reached the electronics

Cracked housings and perished seals let condensation onto the controller board. Symptoms: flickering, intermittent operation, or corrosion visible in the battery compartment. Light corrosion can be cleaned off contacts; a soaked board usually ends the fixture's story.

5. The switch is off

Undignified but common: many fixtures ship with a small ON/OFF switch hidden under the head, and it gets bumped during cleaning. Check it before diagnosing anything else expensive.

When to replace instead

If the battery is sealed in, the housing has failed, or the fixture was a throwaway to begin with, replace it — and this time pick one that will not fail the same way, using the buyer's guide checklist, starting with a replaceable battery.
